ECO 324

Economics of Capital Markets

A nine-week course on the economic foundations, institutions, instruments and mechanisms of capital markets. It connects theory with market behaviour and places particular emphasis on Ghana’s capital market, the Ghana Stock Exchange, regulation, government securities and emerging-market development.

Learning focus

What students should be able to do.

  • Explain the structure and functioning of primary and secondary capital markets.
  • Evaluate the role of financial institutions, instruments and regulation in economic development.
  • Analyse interest rates, risk, return and asset pricing.
  • Apply valuation principles to bonds, equities and selected derivatives.
  • Assess Ghanaian capital-market development in comparative emerging-market perspective.

Course roadmap

Topics and learning progression.

The roadmap below reflects the course outline.

Week 1

Introduction to Capital Markets

Financial systems, economic development and the role of institutions and intermediaries.

Week 2

Financial Instruments

Equities, bonds and derivatives; money-market versus capital-market assets.

Week 3

Primary and Secondary Capital Markets

IPOs, underwriting, securities issuance, exchanges, OTC markets, liquidity and price discovery.

Week 4

Interest Rates and Valuation

Time value of money, interest-rate determination, bond pricing and equity valuation principles.

Week 5

Risk and Return in Capital Markets

Risk-return trade-off, diversification, portfolio theory and the Capital Asset Pricing Model.

Week 6

Debt and Equity Markets

Bond markets, yield curves, risk premiums, interest-rate and default risk, equity dynamics and dividend valuation.

Week 7

Derivative Markets

Futures, options and swaps; hedging, speculation and market implications.

Week 8

Financial Regulation and Market Development

SEC, Bank of Ghana, market supervision, investor protection and Ghana capital-market development.

Week 9

Case Studies and Course Review

Ghanaian capital-market development, challenges and opportunities in African markets, and comparative cases from Kenya and Nigeria.
